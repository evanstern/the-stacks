The Question Journey

What actually happens in the two seconds between hitting Enter and getting an answer with receipts

Two seconds, with receipts

You type “Can a wizard wear armor?” and hit Enter. Two seconds later: an answer, with little [1] [2] footnotes that open the exact paragraph of the rulebook you uploaded.

This course is about everything that happens inside those two seconds — and inside the machine you built. Welcome to Module 1 of 6 of Inside The Stacks.

The Stacks is a self-hosted web app for tabletop RPG game masters: you upload your own rulebooks and campaign notes (Markdown, text, HTML, EPUB, or saved-webpage ZIPs), then ask questions in a chat. Three promises define it:

📎

Ask with receipts

Every factual sentence carries a numbered citation like [1] that opens the exact source passage.

📚

Bring your own books

It answers ONLY from the material you uploaded — your rules, your notes, your table.

🙅

Refuses to bluff

No evidence in your books? It says so, plainly, instead of making something up.

A closed-stacks library, digitized

In an ordinary library you wander the shelves yourself. In a closed-stacks library — which is literally what “the stacks” means — you hand a librarian a question slip at the desk, a runner disappears into the shelves, and your material comes back with call slips pointing at exact pages.

The Stacks is that library, digitized. Six characters staff it — here’s a teaser of the cast (full introductions in Module 2):

🖥️

Web app

The reading room — the page you see and type into. Engineers call the visible part the frontend.

🚪

API

The librarian at the front desk. An API is the backend program every request goes through.

🛠️

Worker

The intake clerk in the back room — a worker processes your uploaded books off-stage.

🗃️

Postgres

The card catalog and ledger. Postgres is a database that remembers every book, chat, and answer.

🧭

Qdrant

The runner in the stacks. Qdrant finds passages by meaning, not by exact words.

🤖

OpenAI

The scholar on call — an LLM that turns text into numbers and drafts the answers.

One question, eight hops

Here’s the module’s big idea: an app is not one program. It’s a relay race of small programs passing messages — and each hop is understandable on its own.

When you hit Enter, your question rides an HTTP request to the front desk, carrying your session cookie. The whole pattern is called RAG. Click through the race yourself:

The code behind the hops

Let’s ground the metaphor in real code — the exact code in this project, untouched. First, the TypeScript function that fires when you hit Enter, from apps/web/app/routes/chat.tsx:

CODE · chat.tsx 

  async function handleSubmit(event: ChatSubmitEvent) {
    event.preventDefault();
    const content = draft.trim();
    if (!content || isSending) {
      return;
    }

    setError(null);
    setDraft("");
    setOptimisticQuestion({
      id: "pending-user-message",
      chat_session_id: session.id,
      role: "user",
      content,
      metadata: {},
      citations: [],
      created_at: new Date().toISOString(),
    });

    try {
      await sendSessionMessage(session.id, content);
      await revalidator.revalidate();
      formRef.current?.reset();
    } catch (sendError) {
      setDraft(content);
      setError(sendError instanceof Error ? sendError.message : "The archive could not answer just now.");
    } finally {
      setOptimisticQuestion(null);
    }
  }
PLAIN ENGLISH

When you hit send, run this. async means it can wait on the network without freezing the page.

Stop the browser’s ancient default habit of reloading the whole page on form submit.

Grab what you typed, minus stray spaces at the ends.

If the box is empty — or a previous question is still in flight — quietly do nothing.

Clear any old error, and empty the input box.

Now the trick: instantly paint a stand-in copy of your message on screen…

…with a made-up ID, pending-user-message. The server hasn’t even been contacted yet.

Stamp it with your session, your role (“user”), your text, and the current time.

NOW actually send the question to the API — and wait for the full answer.

When it returns, re-fetch the conversation so the real messages replace the stand-in.

If anything failed: put your draft back in the box — nothing you typed is lost…

…and show a polite, human error instead of a scary technical one.

Either way, remove the stand-in message. The real one has taken its place — or your draft is back.

That stand-in message is called optimistic UI: show your message instantly, before the server confirms, so the app feels fast. Now hop to the other end of the wire — the Python backend, from apps/api/app/chat_session_service.py:

🤫
Honesty is cheaper

The app would rather say “I don’t know” than make something up — and it doesn’t even bother (or pay for) the AI call when there’s no evidence. Five lines of code, and the whole “refuses to bluff” promise is kept.

It’s theater (the good kind)

💡
Perceived vs. actual progress

That soothing “Searching the archive… Reading sources… Composing answer…” ticker in the chat? Theater. The real work is ONE request that returns the whole answer at once — the code even admits it: “Local preparation estimates shown while the request is pending” (apps/web/app/routes/chat.tsx:494-536). Great apps manage your feeling of progress separately from the progress itself.

Why does naming the hops matter? Because now you can point an AI assistant at the right one — “that’s a backend retrieval problem, not a UI problem” — and know where to look when something breaks. Prove it to yourself:

1. You hit Enter and your question appears in the chat instantly — but the answer takes two seconds. Why does your own message show up immediately?

2. The chat replies “no evidence found” to every single question — even easy ones. Which hop of the journey would you suspect FIRST?

3. A friend wants the app to ALSO answer from general knowledge when the books don’t cover something. Which step of the journey would have to change?
